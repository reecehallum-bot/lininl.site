About

I'm Reece — songwriter, topliner, and producer based in Aotearoa New Zealand.

I work with artists who know what they want to say — but need the right collaborator to shape it into something that lands.

Most projects start as a feeling, a fragment, or a strong instinct. My role is to find the clearest version of that idea — then build the song around it.

I think in scenes, tension, and payoff — not just parts of a song. The goal isn't just to write something that sounds good, but something that holds attention and actually goes somewhere.

I work across artist records and sync, especially in early-stage projects where the direction hasn't fully settled yet — and where getting that right matters as much as the execution.

Songwriting Lanes

01

Cinematic Emotional Pop

Big feeling, slow-burn tension, and emotional payoff. For artists whose sound lives in the space between pop and something heavier — where the chorus doesn't resolve, it opens. The kind of songwriting that earns its runtime.

02

Conceptual / Left-of-Centre Pop

Sharper concepts, stranger angles, and lyrics with a point of view. Songs built around identity, media, perception, and the stories people perform to survive.

03

Hypnotic Dark Pop

Slow builds, unsettling beauty, and hooks that arrive like a bruise. Songs for artists who want their darkness to feel deliberate — not decorative.

04

Sync & Screen

Songs built to carry a scene without explaining it. For trailers, title sequences, TV drama, and brand campaigns where the music has to do the emotional heavy-lifting. Delivered as stems, with licensing terms discussed upfront. Sync briefs welcome — the stranger the brief, the better. Open to both direct briefs and referrals from music supervisors.

How a Project Starts

01 You reach out with something — a reference, a rough idea, a feeling you can't shake yet.
02 We have a short conversation: where the project is, what it needs, what you want to avoid.
03 I come back with a first draft — a topline sketch, a structural demo, or a produced reference.
04 We iterate, usually two to four rounds, until it's right.
05 You leave with a finished work, stems, and clear terms on ownership and usage.
