About Reece

I’m Reece — songwriter, topliner, and producer based in Aotearoa New Zealand. I work across co-writing, sync licensing, artist development, and music marketing strategy — usually with independent artists who know what they want to say but haven’t yet found the clearest version of it.

Where it started

I grew up playing keyboard — arranging pop songs note-for-note until they worked in a room. The goal wasn’t to cover songs. It was to understand them: why that chord, why that melody, why the chorus lands the way it does. I remember playing “I Want It That Way” while the whole school sang along. That was the first time I understood what a song that holds attention actually does.

You don’t learn that from theory. You learn it from watching a room respond to something you built — and then figuring out why.

How it shapes the work

That instinct carried into everything that followed. I think in scenes, tension, and payoff — not just parts or sounds. The question I keep returning to is: what is this song actually doing? Not how does it feel, but what is happening in it, and where is it going? A song that describes an emotion is different from a song that creates one. Most of the work is closing that gap.

Working in film and TV sharpened that further. Music for picture has to work without being noticed — it has to hold the scene, not explain it. That work put me in contact with music supervisors, and those relationships shape how I approach sync now: write to the emotional logic of the picture, not to the brief as a checklist. That’s a different kind of listening than most sessions require, and it bleeds back into how I write songs that aren’t for sync at all.

The other side of the industry

My background also runs through marketing and communications — understanding how messages reach people, why some land and most don’t, and what it takes for something to hold attention in a crowded space. That thinking applies directly to music: how a song has to function before it reaches anyone, what an artist needs to represent before a single release compounds, and where energy actually matters versus where it disappears. It’s why the artist development and music marketing work exists — not as an add-on, but as the same problem approached from a different angle.

I’m based in Aotearoa and work remotely with artists worldwide. If something here connects to what you’re working on, that’s worth a conversation.
