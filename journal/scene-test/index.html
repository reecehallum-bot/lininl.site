Why Most Songs Fail the “Scene Test”

Most songs sound fine.

Good production. Decent melody. Lyrics that kind of make sense.

But nothing happens.

You listen, and by the end of it, you’re in the exact same place you started. No shift. No moment. No memory.

That’s what I think of as failing the scene test.

What is the “scene test”?

If you stripped the production away… could you point to a specific moment and say:

“This is what’s happening.”

Not what the song is about. Not the general vibe.

A moment. Something you could film. Something a character could do or say.

If you can’t picture it, it probably isn’t there.

The problem with most pop songwriting: feelings without behaviour

A lot of songs live entirely in internal language:

“I miss you” “I’m broken” “I can’t let go” “You meant everything”

They’re not wrong. They’re just… unplayable.

There’s no action. No setting. No interaction.

It’s like watching a film where the character just narrates how they feel for three minutes.

What passes the scene test?

A song passes when it gives you something you can see. Not just emotion — behaviour.

Instead of:

“I miss you”

You get:

“I still take the long way home past your street”

Instead of:

“You don’t care anymore”

You get:

“You read the message and changed the subject”

Now we’re in a scene. There’s a place, an action, an implied relationship. The emotion is still there — it’s just embedded.

Why this matters (more than you think)

Scenes create:

  1. Memory. We remember images and moments, not abstract statements.
  2. Relatability. People don’t need the exact same story — they just need something specific enough to map onto their own life.
  3. Movement. A scene implies change. Something is happening, even if it’s subtle.

Without that, songs feel like loops.

The hidden layer: songs should move

Most songs repeat the same idea three times. Verse → chorus → verse → chorus. Same message, different phrasing.

But the best songs shift.

The chorus might mean something different by the second time you hear it. The character reveals something. Or contradicts themselves. Or realises something they didn’t at the start.

That’s not just songwriting. That’s storytelling.

A simple test you can use

Take any song you’ve written and ask:

What is the moment? What is the action? What would the camera see?

If the answer is unclear, you don’t need better lyrics. You need a scene.

How this shapes how I write and co-write

I tend to approach songs like small narrative systems. Something starts. Something unfolds. Something lands.

Not every song needs to be cinematic. But every song needs something to happen.

The shift: how to write more specific songs

You don’t need more complex writing. You need:

fewer generalisations more behaviour clearer moments

Because the goal isn’t to describe a feeling. It’s to place someone inside it.

If a listener can’t picture anything… they won’t stay.

If you’ve got a song that sounds right but you can’t quite picture what’s happening in it — that’s usually the gap I help close.
