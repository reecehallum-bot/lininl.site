Why Your Lyrics Sound Generic (And How to Fix It)

Most lyrics aren’t bad.

They just don’t belong to anyone.

They could be yours.
Or mine.
Or anyone who’s ever tried to write a song about feeling something.

They say the right things.
They sound like songs.

But they don’t stick.

So why do lyrics sound generic — even when they’re technically “good”?

Why your lyrics sound generic

It usually comes down to one thing:

You’re writing feelings without behaviour.

Lines like:

“I miss you” “I can’t let go” “You meant everything”

These aren’t wrong.

They’re just interchangeable.

There’s no context.
No action.
No moment.

Nothing that makes the line yours.

The real problem: abstraction

Generic lyrics live in abstraction.

They describe emotional states without showing what those states look like in the real world.

It’s the difference between:

“I’m heartbroken”

and

“I sat outside your house for an hour and didn’t go in”

One tells you the feeling.
The other puts you inside it.

That’s the gap.

Why generic lyrics feel empty

When there’s no action, there’s nothing to hold onto.

No image.
No movement.
No memory.

The listener understands what you mean — but they don’t experience anything.

And if nothing happens, there’s nothing to remember.

That’s why so many songs feel like they blur together.

What good lyrics do instead

Strong lyrics don’t just express emotion.

They embed emotion inside a moment.

There’s something happening:

a decision a reaction a line of dialogue a small, specific behaviour

Something a listener could picture.

Something that could be filmed.

If your line can’t pass that test, it probably won’t stand out.

Examples: generic vs memorable lyrics

Generic:

“I miss you”

Specific:

“I still take the long way home past your street”

Generic:

“You don’t care anymore”

Specific:

“You read the message and changed the subject”

Generic:

“I can’t move on”

Specific:

“I deleted your number and memorised it anyway”

The emotion hasn’t changed.

The delivery has.

Now there’s a moment.
Now there’s something to remember.

The hidden trap: trying to be relatable

Most generic lyrics come from trying to be universal.

You strip away the details so more people can connect.

But the opposite is true.

The more specific a lyric is, the more people see themselves in it.

Because real experiences aren’t abstract.

They’re messy.
Detailed.
Personal.

That’s what makes them feel true.

How to fix generic lyrics

If you want to stop writing generic lyrics, don’t aim for better words.

Aim for clearer moments.

Start with this:

  1. Replace statements with actions. Instead of saying how someone feels, show what they do.
  2. Anchor the line in something real. A place. A time. An object. A behaviour.
  3. Ask: what would a camera see? If the answer is unclear, the line isn’t grounded yet.
  4. Look for interaction. Someone speaking. Avoiding. Reacting. Choosing. That’s where emotion becomes visible.

The shift

You don’t need deeper feelings.

You need something to happen.

Because the reason your lyrics sound generic isn’t that you’re not saying enough.

It’s that nothing in them can be seen.

And if a listener can’t picture anything, they won’t remember anything.

If you’ve got lyrics that feel right but don’t quite land — that’s usually the gap I help close.
