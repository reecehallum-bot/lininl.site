How to Promote a New Song

Most music promotion starts with an announcement: a release date, a cover image, a caption that says “out now.” Announcing tells people a song exists. Promoting gives them a reason to care before they’ve heard it — and a reason to find it after. These are different problems, and the mechanics change at each stage of the release campaign: before the track drops, on release day, and in the weeks after.

Before the release — seed context, not hype

The pre-release window isn’t for building anticipation around a release date. It’s for establishing the emotional territory the song occupies — who it’s for, what it gives them, when they’d reach for it. A clip that shows what a lyric means in context. A video built around the production before the full track is available. Something that lets a stranger feel what the song is for without having to take a chance on an unfamiliar artist.

TikTok creator seeding — sending the audio to creators before the release — only works if the timing is right. A creator posting two weeks after your release has missed the algorithmic window. Seed creators during the 4–2 week window, with a clear request to post on or around release day.

Pre-saves do two things: they guarantee a stream and a Spotify follow on release day, and they create a small warm audience to notify when the track goes live. The list compounds across releases — every pre-save is a subscriber to the next one.

Release day and the first 72 hours

The first 72 hours are when Spotify runs its most active test of whether to extend the track’s reach. The signal it’s reading isn’t stream count — it’s saves, completion rate, and the ratio of saves to streams. A listener who streams and moves on is counted differently than a listener who saves and comes back. That distinction determines whether the track gets pushed further.

On release day, send to your email list with a specific ask: save the track, not just listen. Most artists ask people to “check it out” — that phrasing produces streams, not saves. The ask has to be explicit. Post across your social channels, engage actively in the first 24 hours — replies, reshares, direct messages to people who’d genuinely care — and watch the saves-to-streams ratio in Spotify for Artists. If it’s low, adjust the ask in remaining posts.

The second wave — weeks 2–4

Most artists stop promoting after the first week. That’s when independent curators become the right target — unlike editorial, they can add tracks after release, and they respond better when there’s live data to share. Save rate, stream count, which algorithmic playlists the track has appeared in. Evidence is more persuasive than a pitch written before anyone’s heard it.

Repurpose content that worked. A clip that earned saves becomes a Reel. A comment thread that surfaced an unexpected reaction becomes a video response. You don’t need new creative output — you need to extract more surface area from what’s already performing.

If the track is sync-ready, start library submissions now. Sync placement moves slowly — conversations started in weeks 2–4 can result in placements months later.

What to actually measure

Stream count in isolation is a vanity metric. The numbers that tell you whether a release is actually working:

  • Save rate — saves divided by streams. Above 10% is a signal the algorithm responds to.
  • Completion rate — what percentage of listeners finish the track. Low completion rates get penalised in recommendations.
  • Follower conversion — how many new Spotify followers came from this release? A track that grows your audience compounds into the next release.

A track with 500 streams and a 20% save rate is outperforming a track with 5,000 streams and a 3% save rate — from the algorithm’s perspective. Build your promotion strategy around the metrics that move the algorithm, not the ones that look good in a screenshot.

Common questions

Start building context 4–8 weeks before release — that’s when you seed TikTok creators, set up your pre-save, and submit to Spotify editorial. Promotion that begins on release day has already missed the most valuable window.

Context-building before the release, a direct save ask to your email list on release day, and independent curator outreach in weeks 2–4 with live performance data. The combination of pre-release seeding and post-release follow-through outperforms any single tactic.

Ask directly. Most artists ask people to “listen” or “check out” a track. Saves require a specific ask — “save this” or “add it to your library” — because the behaviour is different from passively streaming. Your email list and engaged social followers are the highest-conversion audience for that ask.

Four to six weeks post-release is the useful active promotion window. The first 72 hours matter most for algorithmic signals; weeks 2–4 are the right window for independent curator outreach with live data. After six weeks, effort is better directed toward the next release.
